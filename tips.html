Expert Tips to Reduce Food Waste

Small changes in how we shop, store, and consume food can make a huge difference

Smart Shopping Strategies

  • Create a meal plan before shopping to buy only what you need
  • Make a detailed shopping list and stick to it—avoid impulse purchases
  • Shop your fridge and pantry first to use what you already have
  • Buy "ugly" or imperfect produce—it's just as nutritious and flavorful
  • Avoid bulk purchases unless you're certain you'll use everything
  • Check expiration dates and choose products you'll use soonest
  • Shop more frequently with smaller trips to buy fresher food
  • Don't shop when hungry—you'll buy more than you need

Proper Food Storage

  • Store potatoes, onions, and garlic in cool, dark, dry places—not the fridge
  • Keep herbs fresh by storing them upright in water like flowers
  • Separate bananas from other fruits—they release ethylene gas that speeds ripening
  • Wrap celery and broccoli in foil to keep them crisp for weeks
  • Store leafy greens with a paper towel to absorb excess moisture
  • Use airtight containers to extend the life of leftovers and produce
  • Freeze bread, herbs, and overripe fruits for later use
  • Organize your fridge with older items in front using "first in, first out"

Understanding Date Labels

  • "Best Before" means quality may decline, but food is still safe to eat
  • "Use By" indicates the last date for optimal safety—follow this for meat and dairy
  • Most foods are safe beyond these dates—trust your senses (smell, look, taste)
  • Canned goods can last years past the printed date if stored properly
  • Eggs are typically good for 3-5 weeks after purchase
  • Dairy products often last a week or more past "best before" dates
  • Dry goods like pasta and rice can last indefinitely if kept dry

Meal Planning & Prep

  • Plan weekly meals around what you already have in your pantry
  • Choose recipes that share ingredients to minimize waste
  • Cook the right portions—use measuring tools or serving guides
  • Prep ingredients in advance so you're more likely to cook at home
  • Designate one day per week as "leftover day" to clear the fridge
  • Double recipes and freeze extra portions for future meals
  • Transform leftovers into new dishes (e.g., roast chicken → soup → sandwich)

Creative Uses for Food Scraps

  • Make vegetable stock from onion skins, carrot tops, and celery ends
  • Blend overripe fruits into smoothies, sauces, or baked goods
  • Turn stale bread into croutons, breadcrumbs, or bread pudding
  • Pickle vegetable scraps for extended shelf life and added flavor
  • Regrow green onions, lettuce, and herbs from kitchen scraps
  • Use citrus peels for cleaning solutions or zest for recipes
  • Freeze coffee grounds for garden compost or natural cleaning scrubs
  • Dry herbs and vegetable peels to make seasoning blends

Portion Control

  • Serve smaller portions—you can always get more if needed
  • Use smaller plates to naturally reduce portion sizes
  • When dining out, ask for a takeaway box immediately and save half
  • Share large restaurant meals with dining companions
  • Cook in batches and freeze individual portions for convenience
  • Learn standard serving sizes to avoid cooking too much
  • Save leftovers immediately after meals—don't let them sit out

Freezing for Freshness

  • Freeze overripe bananas for smoothies and baking
  • Batch cook and freeze complete meals in portion sizes
  • Flash freeze berries on trays before storing in bags
  • Freeze fresh herbs in olive oil in ice cube trays
  • Store leftover wine in ice cube trays for cooking
  • Label and date all frozen items to track freshness
  • Use freezer-safe containers and remove excess air to prevent freezer burn
  • Keep a freezer inventory list to remember what you have

Composting at Home

  • Start a compost bin for vegetable scraps, fruit peels, and eggshells
  • Include coffee grounds and tea bags in your compost
  • Balance "green" materials (food scraps) with "brown" materials (dry leaves, paper)
  • Turn your compost regularly to speed decomposition
  • Keep meat, dairy, and oils out of compost to avoid pests
  • Use finished compost to enrich your garden soil naturally
  • Try vermicomposting (worm composting) for apartment living
  • Consider bokashi composting for all food scraps including meat
